Chapter 1

The Human Question

How we've always found answers — by asking other humans.

Since the beginning of time, when humans had a question, they asked another human — a subject matter expert.

Think about it:

  • You feel sick → You go to a doctor
  • Your car breaks down → You call a mechanic
  • You need legal advice → You talk to a lawyer
  • You want to learn to cook → You ask your mom or grandma

This is the oldest form of getting answers: finding someone who knows a lot about a specific topic and asking them directly.

Example: Planning a Trip to Japan

Imagine you want to plan a two-week trip to Japan. In the "human expert" era, here's what you'd do:

  1. Ask a friend who visited Japan → "What should I see? Where should I stay?"
  2. Visit a travel agent → They'd plan your itinerary, book hotels, arrange flights
  3. Buy a guidebook → Lonely Planet or Fodor's Japan guide
  4. Ask a coworker who is Japanese → "What food should I try? Any tips?"

How long would this take? Weeks. Maybe a month of gathering opinions, comparing advice, and putting it all together yourself.

The Limitations of Asking Humans

This system worked, but it had problems:

ProblemExample
AccessYou might not know a doctor, lawyer, or expert personally
CostExperts charge money — $200/hour for a lawyer, $150 for a mechanic
AvailabilityYour friend is busy, the travel agent is on vacation
BiasYour friend hates sushi, so they tell you to skip it — but you might love it
Limited knowledgeEven experts don't know everything
Key Takeaway

For thousands of years, getting answers = finding the right person to ask. This was slow, expensive, and not always possible.

Exercise 1.1

Think of three questions you had in the last month. For each one, write down:

  • What was the question?
  • Who did you ask (or who would you have asked)?
  • How long did it take to get a good answer?

Example: Question: "What's the best phone plan for my family?" — Who I asked: My brother-in-law who works in telecom — How long: Had to wait until Sunday dinner to ask him, 5 days.

Exercise 1.2

Think about a time you needed expert help but couldn't get it. Maybe you couldn't afford a lawyer, or didn't know a good plumber. Write it down. We'll come back to this later.

Chapter 2

The Search Engine Era

How Google changed everything — and what it still couldn't do.

In the late 1990s, Google appeared. Suddenly, instead of asking a person, you could type words into a box and get information from the entire internet. This was magical. But it wasn't perfect.

How Google Works (The Simple Version)

Think of Google as a giant library with a really fast librarian.

  1. Billions of websites exist on the internet (the books in the library)
  2. Google's robots crawl the internet and make a catalog of every webpage (the librarian reads every book)
  3. When you type words into Google, the librarian finds the most relevant pages (pulls books off the shelf)
  4. You get a list of 10 blue links per page (the librarian hands you a stack of books)

But here's the catch: the librarian hands you the books. YOU still have to read them.

Example: Planning a Trip to Japan (Google Era)
  1. Search "best places to visit in Japan" → 500 million results. Click the first 5 links. Read 5 different articles. Take notes.
  2. Search "best hotels in Tokyo" → Compare TripAdvisor, Booking.com, travel blogs. Spend 2 hours reading reviews.
  3. Search "Japan itinerary 2 weeks" → Find 3 different itineraries. None are exactly what you want. Mix and match.
  4. Search "Japan visa requirements", "best time to visit Japan", "Japan train pass worth it"...

How long does this take? Still days. You have 40 browser tabs open. Notes scattered everywhere. Conflicting information. And you STILL have to put it all together into YOUR plan.

The Google Problem: Information vs. Answers

Google gives you information. It does NOT give you answers.

What You WantWhat Google Gives You
"A training program for new employees"50 articles ABOUT training programs
"A birthday speech for my dad"Tips on HOW to write a speech
"A meal plan for losing weight"100 different diet websites
"A budget for my small business"Templates and articles about budgeting

Google points you to information, but you still do the heavy lifting: reading multiple sources, comparing, filtering, organizing, and creating the final thing yourself.

Example: Building a Training Program

Your boss asks you to create a training program for new sales hires. With Google:

  1. Search "sales training program template" → Find 20 templates, none fit your company
  2. Search "onboarding best practices" → Read 10 articles
  3. Search "sales training exercises" → Find scattered ideas
  4. Search "how to measure training effectiveness" → More articles
  5. Open PowerPoint and spend 3 days building it from scratch

Google helped you gather ingredients. But you still had to cook the meal.

Key Takeaway

Google was a massive leap forward — free access to the world's information, instantly. But it still required you to know the right keywords, read through many results, judge quality, and assemble everything yourself.

Notice: How many results? Did any single result give you EXACTLY what you needed? How many tabs did you open? How long would it take to build your personal plan from these results?

You type:

Instead of 3 days of research and building, you have a solid first draft in 2 minutes.

Chapter 4

How AI Actually Works

LLMs explained simply — no math, no code.

When people say "AI" today, they usually mean a Large Language Model (LLM). Let's break that down: Large = read billions of pages. Language = works with human language. Model = a program that learned patterns from all that text.

The Restaurant Analogy

Imagine a chef who has:

  • Eaten at every restaurant in the world
  • Read every cookbook ever written
  • Watched every cooking show ever made

You say: "Make me a pasta that's creamy but not heavy, with lemon, no dairy."

The chef doesn't look up a recipe. They've absorbed so many patterns about food that they can create a new dish matching your description. They know "creamy without dairy" means cashew cream or coconut milk. They know lemon pairs with certain herbs.

That's how an LLM works. It has "read" so much text that it can generate new text following the patterns it learned.

The Core Mechanic: Next-Word Prediction

An LLM predicts the next word. That's it. It's a very sophisticated word-prediction machine.

When you type "The capital of France is", the LLM has seen this pattern thousands of times and predicts the next word is "Paris."

Your Phone's AutocompleteAI / LLM
Learned from YOUR textsLearned from the ENTIRE internet
Predicts 1-3 wordsGenerates pages of text
Simple patternsComplex understanding
Can't follow instructionsFollows detailed instructions

What Does "Training" Mean?

Training a dog: Show the dog hundreds of examples: "Sit" → reward when it sits. Over time, the dog learns the pattern. It doesn't understand English. It learned the pattern between the sound and the action.

Training an AI: Engineers show the AI billions of examples of text. The AI finds patterns: what words follow what words, how sentences are structured, how questions are answered. It learned incredibly complex patterns.

AI Doesn't "Know" Things

AI doesn't have a database of facts. It has learned patterns from text. This is why it can sometimes be wrong — we call these "hallucinations."

You ask: "Who won the Super Bowl in 2028?" The AI might confidently make up an answer because it has seen the pattern "Who won the Super Bowl in [year]" thousands of times, and it always ends with a team name. So it predicts a team name — even for a year that hasn't happened.

This is not the AI lying. It's following patterns without knowing what's true or false.

Key Takeaway

LLMs are sophisticated pattern-matching machines. They've learned from billions of texts and generate new text following those patterns. They don't "think" — they predict. This is their superpower (write about anything) and their weakness (confidently wrong sometimes).

Chapter 5

Talking to AI Effectively

The art of asking good questions — prompt engineering made simple.

Getting great results from AI is like giving instructions to a new employee. The clearer and more specific you are, the better the result. Vague question = Vague answer. Specific question = Specific answer.
Bad PromptGood Prompt
"Tell me about marketing""Explain 5 low-cost marketing strategies for a local bakery that just opened, with examples"
"Write an email""Write a professional email to a client explaining a 2-week delay due to supply chain issues. Apologetic but confident."
"Help with Excel""How do I create a pivot table showing total sales by product by month? Data is in columns A (date), B (product), C (amount)."

The 5 Elements of a Great Prompt

1. Role — Tell the AI WHO to be

"You are an experienced nutritionist..." "Act as a patient kindergarten teacher..." "You are a professional resume writer..."

2. Task — Tell the AI WHAT to do

"...create a 7-day meal plan..." "...explain photosynthesis..." "...rewrite my resume..."

3. Context — Give BACKGROUND

"...for a 40-year-old man with type 2 diabetes who hates cooking..." "...for a 5-year-old who loves dinosaurs..." "...I'm applying for a senior marketing role at a tech company..."

4. Format — Tell the AI HOW to present it

"...in a table with columns for breakfast, lunch, dinner, and snacks" "...using a story about a dinosaur" "...in bullet points, keeping it to one page"

5. Constraints — Set LIMITS

"...no seafood, under 2000 calories per day" "...use words a 5-year-old would understand" "...don't use buzzwords, be specific and honest"
Example: Building the Perfect Prompt Step by Step

Start vague: "Write a speech"

Add Role: "You are a professional speechwriter. Write a speech"

Add Task: "...Write a 5-minute best man speech"

Add Context: "...The groom's name is Tom, I've been his best friend since college, we were roommates for 3 years. He met his wife Sarah at a cooking class."

Add Format: "...Include 2-3 funny anecdotes, a heartfelt moment, and end with a toast."

Add Constraints: "...Keep it PG-rated, nothing embarrassing. Warm and funny, not roast-style."

Common Prompt Patterns

PatternExample
"Explain Like I'm...""Explain blockchain like I'm a 10-year-old"
"Give Me Options""Give me 5 ways to start a presentation, from serious to humorous"
"Step-by-Step""Walk me through changing a tire, assuming I've never done it"
"Review/Improve""Here's my cover letter. Tell me what's weak and rewrite those parts"
"Transform""Turn these meeting notes into a professional summary email"
Key Takeaway

AI is only as good as what you ask it. Clear prompts with role, task, context, format, and constraints is the single most valuable AI skill. And you can always refine through follow-up conversation.

Chapter 6

AI Beyond Chat

Images, audio, video, code, and data — AI can do more than write text.

Modern AI can work with images (create and understand pictures), audio (transcribe and generate speech), code (write programs), and data (analyze spreadsheets). When an AI works with multiple types, it's called multimodal.

AI and Images

Creating Images From Words

You type a description, AI creates an image. No photographer, no Photoshop, no stock photo website.

"A golden retriever wearing a tiny business suit, sitting at a desk in a corporate office, looking very serious, photorealistic style"

Understanding Images

You can show AI a photo and ask questions:

  • Upload a photo of a dashboard warning light → "What does this mean?"
  • Upload a photo of a plant → "What kind of plant is this?"
  • Upload a screenshot of an error → "How do I fix this?"

AI and Audio

  • Transcription: Upload a meeting recording → AI types out everything said
  • Translation: Speak in English → AI outputs Spanish
  • Voice generation: Type text → AI reads it aloud naturally

AI and Code

Even if you're not a programmer, this is useful:

  • "Create an Excel formula that averages column B only where column A says 'Sales'" → AI writes the formula
  • "I have this error on my screen [paste it]" → AI explains what's wrong

AI and Data

  • Upload a spreadsheet → "What are the top 3 trends?"
  • Paste a table → "Create a chart showing growth by quarter"
  • Share financial data → "Summarize this in 5 bullet points for my boss"

Multimodal AI

Old AIMultimodal AI
A pen pal who can only write lettersA colleague who can read docs, look at photos, listen to recordings, and respond in any format
Key Takeaway

AI isn't just a chatbot. It can see, hear, create images, write code, and analyze data. This makes it useful in almost every field.

Chapter 7

AI That Takes Action

Agentic AI — from advisor to doer.

Everything so far is AI that responds. You ask, it answers. But what if AI could actually do things for you — not just write about tasks, but complete them? This is Agentic AI.

The Assistant Analogy

Regular AI (Advisor)Agentic AI (Executive Assistant)
"How should I organize this event?"
AI: "Here's a plan: book a venue, send invitations..."
You still do everything		"Organize this event"
AI: Books the venue, sends invitations, orders catering, follows up on RSVPs...
The work gets DONE

What Makes AI "Agentic"?

  1. Breaks down complex tasks into steps
  2. Takes actions — sends emails, searches databases, creates files
  3. Makes decisions — if the venue is booked, tries a different one
  4. Uses tools — accesses the internet, uses software
  5. Works autonomously — keeps going without micromanagement
Example: Research Report

Regular AI: Writes a report from memory (potentially outdated).

Agentic AI:

  1. Searches the internet for competitors' latest news
  2. Visits their websites to check current pricing
  3. Reads their recent press releases
  4. Checks review sites for customer feedback
  5. Writes a report with current, accurate information
  6. Saves it to your Google Drive

How Agentic AI Works: The Loop

1 PLAN — "I need to find competitor pricing"
↓
2 ACT — Searches the competitor's website
↓
3 OBSERVE — "The pricing page has changed"
↓
4 ADJUST — "Let me check other competitors too"
↓
5 REPEAT until the task is complete
Key Takeaway

Agentic AI shifts from "AI as an advisor" to "AI as a doer." It breaks down complex tasks, uses tools, makes decisions, and delivers completed results.

Chapter 8

AI Connects to Your World

MCPs and tool use — giving AI hands to work with.

For AI to actually do things, it needs to connect to the tools you use — email, calendar, databases, files. MCPs (Model Context Protocol) are standardized bridges that make this possible.

The Translator Analogy

Imagine you hire a brilliant assistant who speaks every language. But they're locked in a soundproof room with no phone, no computer. No matter how smart they are, they can't check your email or look at your calendar.

MCPs are like giving that assistant a phone, a computer, and keys to the office.

Think of It Like USB Ports

Your computer has USB ports. You can plug in a keyboard, mouse, printer, camera — each device is different, but they all connect through the same standard port.

MCPs are like USB ports for AI. A standard way to "plug in" to different services:

MCP ConnectionWhat It Lets AI Do
Gmail MCPRead, search, write, and send emails
Calendar MCPView, create, and modify events
Google Drive MCPRead, create, and organize documents
Slack MCPSend messages, read channels
Database MCPQuery and analyze company data
Web Search MCPSearch the internet for current info
Example: With and Without MCPs

Without MCPs:

You: "What meetings do I have tomorrow?"
AI: "I'm sorry, I don't have access to your calendar."

With MCPs:

You: "What meetings do I have tomorrow?"
AI: "You have 3 meetings: 9 AM Team standup, 11 AM Client call with Acme Corp, 2 PM 1:1 with your manager. Want me to prepare talking points for the client call?"

Example: Full Workflow With MCPs

You say: "Prepare me for my meeting with Acme Corp tomorrow"

1 Calendar MCP → Finds meeting details (time, attendees, agenda)
2 Email MCP → Searches recent emails with Acme Corp
3 CRM MCP → Pulls account info, deal status
4 Drive MCP → Finds the proposal you sent last month
5 Web MCP → Checks Acme Corp's latest news

Result: A complete briefing with talking points, context from emails, and a heads-up that Acme just announced a new CEO. All in 30 seconds.

Why MCPs Matter

Before MCPsAfter MCPs
Each AI + tool combo needs custom codingStandard connection works everywhere
Only big tech could build integrationsAnyone can create an MCP server
AI tools isolated from your dataAI seamlessly works with your tools
Key Takeaway

MCPs are bridges connecting AI to the real world — your email, calendar, files, databases. Without MCPs, AI is a smart brain with no hands. With MCPs, AI becomes a capable assistant that can interact with your tools and get things done.

Chapter 10

Using AI Responsibly

Limitations, ethics, and best practices — being the smart human behind the smart machine.

AI is powerful, but like any powerful tool, it needs to be used responsibly. A chainsaw can build a house or cause harm — it depends on who's using it and how.

The 5 Rules of Responsible AI Use

Rule 1: Verify Important Facts

AI can hallucinate — generate false information that sounds real. Always fact-check anything that matters: statistics, dates, legal requirements, medical info.

Think of AI like a very confident intern. Usually right, but you wouldn't sign a contract based solely on what an intern told you.

Rule 2: Don't Share Sensitive Information

When you type into AI, that text is sent to servers. Be careful with:

  • Passwords and login credentials
  • Social security or credit card numbers
  • Confidential business information
  • Personal health information

Rule 3: AI Is a Tool, Not a Replacement for Thinking

GoodBad
Use AI to draft, then review with your expertiseCopy AI's output without reading it
Use AI to brainstorm, then pick the best ideasLet AI make all your decisions

Rule 4: Be Transparent

If you use AI to help create something, be honest about it when appropriate. Many organizations have policies about AI disclosure.

Rule 5: Understand the Biases

AI learned from human-created text, which contains human biases. Be aware of this, especially when using AI for hiring, evaluations, or decisions affecting people.

What AI Can and Cannot Do

AI CANAI CANNOT
Generate text quicklyGuarantee accuracy
Follow instructionsTruly understand meaning
Find patterns in dataHave emotions or empathy
Work 24/7Replace human judgment
Help you think through problemsMake ethical decisions for you

The Human + AI Formula

+ Human Expertise + AI Speed = Superpower
+ You know WHAT needs to be done → AI helps you DO it faster
+ You provide JUDGMENT → AI provides SCALE
+ You make the DECISIONS → AI does the HEAVY LIFTING
Key Takeaway

AI is the most powerful productivity tool since the internet. Use it wisely: verify facts, protect sensitive data, think critically, be transparent, and remember — AI is your assistant, you're still the boss.

Vibe Coding

Building software by describing what you want — no programming knowledge needed.

Vibe coding is writing software by having a conversation with AI. You describe what you want in plain English, and AI writes the code for you. You don't need to know any programming language. You just need to know what you want.

The Interior Designer Analogy

Imagine you want to renovate your kitchen. You have two options:

Traditional WayVibe Coding Way
Learn carpentry, plumbing, electrical wiring, tiling, painting. Do everything yourself.Hire an interior designer. Tell them: "I want a modern kitchen with white cabinets, a marble island, and warm lighting." They build it for you.

Vibe coding is like being the client who describes the vision. AI is the designer + builder who makes it happen. You don't need to know HOW to build it — you need to know WHAT you want.

What Can You Build With Vibe Coding?

  • Websites — Landing pages, portfolios, blogs
  • Web apps — Dashboards, calculators, forms, trackers
  • Automations — Scripts that process data, send emails, generate reports
  • Chrome extensions — Custom browser tools
  • Data tools — Spreadsheet analyzers, chart generators
  • Internal tools — CRM dashboards, inventory trackers, onboarding checklists
Example: Building a Customer ROI Calculator

You tell AI:

"Build me a web-based ROI calculator. The user inputs their current monthly spend on manual processes, number of employees, and hours saved per week. It should calculate annual savings and show a nice chart. Make it look professional with a clean, modern design."

AI writes all the HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. You open the file in your browser and you have a working calculator. The entire process takes about 5 minutes.

Example: Building an Expense Tracker

You tell AI:

"Create a personal expense tracker web app. I want to add expenses with a date, category (food, transport, entertainment, bills), and amount. Show me a running total and a pie chart of spending by category. Save the data in the browser so I don't lose it when I close the tab."

5 minutes later, you have a fully functional expense tracker.

The Vibe Coding Process

1 DESCRIBE what you want in plain English
↓
2 AI WRITES the code
↓
3 You TEST it (open in browser, run it)
↓
4 You REFINE — "Make the button bigger" / "Add a dark mode" / "Fix this bug"
↓
5 REPEAT until it's exactly what you want

Why This Matters

Before vibe coding, if you had an idea for a tool, you had three options:

  1. Learn to code (months/years of study)
  2. Hire a developer ($50-200/hour, weeks of back-and-forth)
  3. Give up on the idea

Now there's a fourth option: describe it to AI and have it built in minutes.

Tips for Effective Vibe Coding

  • Start simple — Get a basic version working first, then add features one at a time
  • Be specific about what you see — "A blue button in the top-right corner" is better than "add a button somewhere"
  • Test after every change — Don't ask for 10 features at once. Add one, test, add the next
  • Use screenshots — If something looks wrong, take a screenshot and show AI: "This doesn't look right, the chart is overlapping the text"
  • Iterate, don't restart — If something is 80% right, refine it. Don't start over
Key Takeaway

Vibe coding lets anyone build software by describing what they want in plain English. You don't need to learn programming — you need to learn to describe your vision clearly. The AI handles the technical implementation.

System Prompts & Custom Instructions

How to configure AI's personality, knowledge, and behavior — and what CLAUDE.md is.

A system prompt is like a job description for AI. Before the conversation starts, it tells the AI who it should be, what it knows, and how it should behave. It's the difference between talking to a generic assistant and talking to YOUR specialized assistant.

The New Employee Analogy

When you hire a new employee, you don't just say "Go work." You give them:

A system prompt does the same thing for AI.

Without system prompt:

You: "Review this email draft"
AI: Gives generic feedback

With system prompt:

System: "You are a communications specialist at a B2B SaaS company selling to enterprise healthcare clients. Our tone is professional but warm. We never use jargon. We always lead with the customer's pain point, not our features. Our company name is MedFlow."

You: "Review this email draft"
AI: Gives feedback specific to your company's style, audience, and communication guidelines

An API (Application Programming Interface) is how software programs talk to each other. When you use Uber, the app uses APIs to talk to the maps service, the payment system, and the driver's phone. Understanding APIs helps you understand how AI connects to the world.

You're sitting at a restaurant. The kitchen can make food, and you want food, but you can't walk into the kitchen yourself. The waiter takes your order, brings it to the kitchen, and brings the food back to you.

When You...APIs Are Working Behind the Scenes

RAG: AI + Your Data

Retrieval Augmented Generation — teaching AI about YOUR specific information.

RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation) is a technique where AI retrieves relevant information from your documents before generating a response. It's like giving AI a cheat sheet of your company's knowledge before it answers your question.

The Open-Book Exam Analogy

Regular AIAI with RAG
A student taking a closed-book exam. They can only use what they memorized (training data). If they didn't study a topic, they guess.A student taking an open-book exam. They have your company's documents right there. They look up the answer before responding.

Why RAG Matters

Regular AI was trained on public internet data. It doesn't know about:

  • Your company's products, pricing, or processes
  • Your internal documentation or policies
  • Your customer data or sales playbooks
  • Your proprietary research or reports

RAG lets you give AI access to this information so it can answer questions accurately.

Example: Customer Support with RAG

Without RAG:

Customer asks: "How do I set up SSO in your product?"
AI: Gives a generic answer about SSO that may not match your product at all.

With RAG:

AI retrieves your product's SSO documentation, then answers with accurate, step-by-step instructions specific to your product, including screenshots references and known gotchas.

Now you can ask:

How RAG Works (Simplified)

1 UPLOAD your documents (PDFs, docs, web pages)
↓
2 AI CHUNKS them into searchable pieces
↓
3 You ask a QUESTION
↓
4 AI RETRIEVES the most relevant chunks
↓
5 AI GENERATES an answer using those chunks as context

  6. Deliver: Posts the digest to a Slack channel

Your sales team gets a beautiful daily briefing without anyone lifting a finger.

An agent triggered when you publish a blog post:

  1. Trigger: New blog post published
  2. Read: Fetches the blog content
  3. Generate: Creates 5 LinkedIn posts, 10 tweets, an email newsletter blurb
  4. Create: Generates a social media image
  5. Queue: Schedules posts across platforms
